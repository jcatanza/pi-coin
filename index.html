§ 01

The Experiment

You have a fair coin. Here is a deceptively simple game:

  1. Toss the coin repeatedly until the number of heads first exceeds the number of tails.
  2. Write down the fraction H/τ — the number of heads divided by the total number of tosses.
  3. Start over and repeat the game many times.
  4. Average all your fractions.

The claim — proved by Jim Propp in a 2026 paper — is that this average converges to a number you know well:

Main Result
Average of H/τ  →  π/4 ≈ 0.7854…

That means if you multiply your average by 4, you get an estimate of π. No circles involved. Just coin flips.

Example trial

Suppose your tosses go: T, H, T, H, H. Tails led after toss 1, were tied after toss 2, tails led again after toss 3, tied again after toss 4, and finally heads won after toss 5. So τ = 5, H = 3, and you record 3/5 = 0.6.

Notice that the game always ends on a head (heads must outnumber tails for the first time), and τ is always odd: 1, 3, 5, 7, … The simplest outcome — one toss, result is H — gives H/τ = 1/1 = 1. This happens with probability 1/2, since the first toss is heads half the time.

§ 02

Why This Should Surprise You

Before looking at any proof, sit with the strangeness of this for a moment. π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. It lives in geometry. Why would it appear in the average of fractions generated by coin flips?

This isn't a coincidence — it's a deep connection between combinatorics (counting paths), probability (random walks), and analysis (series and integrals). Propp's result threads all three together.

Historical context

The most famous geometric π estimator is Buffon's Needle (1777): drop a needle of length L onto a floor ruled with parallel lines spaced L apart. The probability of the needle crossing a line is 2/π. Propp's method is the coin-tossing analogue — purely combinatorial, no geometry required.

The key mathematical object here is a random walk. Think of standing at 0 on a number line. Every coin flip moves you +1 (heads) or −1 (tails). The game stops the first time you step onto +1. The question "what fraction of your steps were in the positive direction?" turns out to encode π in its expected value.

§ 03

The Proof — Step by Step

Here's the full proof, broken into pieces a strong high school student can follow. Each step uses only things you've seen in precalc or early calculus.

Step 1 — Rewrite the fraction

At the stopping time τ, heads outnumber tails by exactly 1. So:

Hτ − Tτ = 1 (heads beat tails by 1) Hτ + Tτ = τ (total tosses) Adding: 2Hτ = τ + 1 → Hτ = (τ+1)/2 Therefore: Hτ/τ = 1/2 + 1/(2τ)

Taking expected values: E[Hτ/τ] = 1/2 + (1/2)·E[1/τ]

The whole problem reduces to computing E[1/τ]. We need to know: on average, how large is 1 divided by the stopping time?

Step 2 — Catalan numbers give us P(τ = 2m−1)

For the walk to first hit +1 at step 2m−1, it must: stay ≤ 0 for 2m−2 steps, return to 0 at step 2m−2, then step to +1. The number of such paths is the Catalan number Cm−1:

Ck = C(2k, k) / (k+1) (the k-th Catalan number) First few: C₀=1, C₁=1, C₂=2, C₃=5, C₄=14, C₅=42, … P(τ = 2m−1) = Cm−1 / 22m−1
What are Catalan numbers?

Ck counts the number of ways to take k steps right and k steps up on a grid without crossing above the diagonal — or equivalently, the number of valid bracket sequences of length 2k, the number of ways to triangulate a polygon, and dozens of other things. They show up everywhere in combinatorics.

Step 3 — Compute E[1/τ] using a clever series

E[1/τ] = Σm≥1 (1/(2m−1)) · Cm−1/4m−1 · (1/2) = (1/2) · Σk≥0 Ck / ((2k+1) · 4k) ← let k = m−1 Call this sum A (we'll evaluate it next).

Step 4 — Turn the sum into an integral

This is the key trick. Use two identities:

  • Ck = C(2k,k)/(k+1)
  • 1/(k+1) = ∫₀¹ tk dt   (just integrate tk from 0 to 1)

Substituting and swapping sum and integral (Tonelli's theorem says this is legal here):

A = ∫₀¹ [Σk≥0 C(2k,k)·tk / ((2k+1)·4k)] dt The sum inside is exactly the Taylor series for arcsin(√t)/√t: arcsin(x) = Σk≥0 C(2k,k)·x2k+1 / ((2k+1)·4k) Setting x = √t: arcsin(√t)/√t = Σk≥0 C(2k,k)·tk / ((2k+1)·4k) So: A = ∫₀¹ arcsin(√t)/√t dt

Step 5 — Evaluate the integral

Substitute u = √t (so t = u², dt = 2u du):

A = ∫₀¹ arcsin(u)/u · 2u du = 2∫₀¹ arcsin(u) du Integrate by parts (let f = arcsin(u), g' = 1): ∫₀¹ arcsin(u) du = [u·arcsin(u)]₀¹ − ∫₀¹ u/√(1−u²) du Boundary term: 1·arcsin(1) − 0 = π/2 Remaining integral: substitute w = 1−u²: ∫₀¹ u/√(1−u²) du = 1 So: A = 2(π/2 − 1) = π − 2 And: E[1/τ] = A/2 = (π − 2)/2 = π/2 − 1

Step 6 — Assemble the result

E[Hτ/τ] = 1/2 + (1/2)·E[1/τ] = 1/2 + (1/2)·(π/2 − 1) = 1/2 + π/4 − 1/2 = π/4 ∎
Bonus insight

Half the time τ = 1 (first toss is heads), giving H/τ = 1. The other half gives H/τ > 1/2. So E[H/τ] lies in (3/4, 1). But E[H/τ] = π/4 ≈ 0.785 — which is indeed between 3/4 and 1. This is a probabilistic proof that 3 < π < 4!

§ 04

What to Take Away

The new thing in this paper

The fact that E[1/τ] = π/2 − 1 was known (it appears in Feller's classic probability textbook, Vol. I). What's new is the realization that E[Hτ/τ] = π/4 — the fraction of heads at stopping time has π/4 as its expected value. Propp found a probabilistic interpretation that nobody had noticed before.

The proof chain is worth memorizing as a pattern, because you'll see each move again:

  1. Algebraic simplification — rewrite E[H/τ] in terms of E[1/τ], a simpler quantity.
  2. Combinatorial counting — use Catalan numbers to get P(τ = 2m−1) exactly.
  3. Integral trick — turn a hard sum into an integral using 1/(k+1) = ∫₀¹ tᵏ dt.
  4. Taylor series recognition — identify the sum inside the integral as arcsin(√t)/√t.
  5. Integration by parts — evaluate ∫ arcsin(u) du, collecting the π term.
Why does π appear?

The arcsin function is where the circle sneaks in. Its Taylor series coefficients involve the same binomial coefficients C(2k,k) that count Catalan paths. The geometry of circles and the combinatorics of coin-toss paths are secretly the same structure — they both encode the same numbers.

The convergence you saw in the demo is slow — error decreases like 1/√N. After 10,000 trials you might get within 0.01 of π. This is the inherent cost of Monte Carlo methods: cheap per sample, but slow to converge. Better algorithms exist for computing π, but none of them are this elegant or this surprising.
